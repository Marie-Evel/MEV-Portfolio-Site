UX/UI CASE STUDY

Explore a city like you've never experienced before!

Street­Cred will take you on a tour of the must-see land­marks, while in­tro­duc­ing you to hidden gems rec­om­mended by the lo­cals. You’ll solve rid­dles to un­veil your next lo­ca­tion, com­plete fun pic­ture chal­lenges, and learn about the his­tory and cul­ture of the city.

Project Overview

  • the problem

StreetCred is a native app I developed as part of an online UX designer certification course I took at CareerFoundry between August 2019 and March 2020. The goal of this case study is to illustrate the UX design process from start to finish within the context of an app-building project.

WHAT'S THE PROBLEM?

Peo­ple like to play games. Peo­ple like to play games on their phones. But peo­ple also want to play with their friends and spend more time out­doors. How about a so­cial scav­enger hunt?

POTENTIAL SOLUTION

I be­lieve that cre­at­ing an app that pro­vides ready-made scav­enger hunts with high-qual­ity con­tent, such as clever rid­dles, fun photo chal­lenges, and in­ter­est­ing his­tor­i­cal info, will en­able users to dis­cover a new city ­­­­­while spend­ing time out­doors and shar­ing the ex­pe­ri­ence with their fam­ily and friends. Add to that a beau­ti­fully de­signed in­ter­face with in­tu­itive nav­i­ga­tion to make for a mem­o­rable sight­see­ing ex­pe­ri­ence.

TARGET AUDIENCE

The tar­get au­di­ence for this app con­sists of late baby boomers, gen­er­a­tion Xers, and mil­len­ni­als be­tween the ages of 18 and 60 years old. Re­gard­less of age, the app con­cept must ap­peal to ad­ven­tur­ous peo­ple who like to travel, and are in­ter­ested in culture and his­tory.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

Before jumping into mockups, wireframes, and user flows, it’s important to know our competitors, identify what they’re doing well, and learn from their weaknesses to improve our own product.

There are a few popular scavenger hunt apps on the market, most of which create custom hunts for large groups or corporations (at a steep price). Others require users to create their own hunts. Very few apps offer ready-made hunts that can be played on demand.

I’ve identified and analyzed two competitors who provide ready-to-play, affordable scavenger hunts: Let’s Roam, and Urban Adventure Quest (UAQ). Let’s take a look at their strengths and weaknesses.

Strengths

  • Hunts available in 350 cities all over the world, including U.S., Europe, Australia, and Asia.
  • Good variety of themed hunts: bar crawls, ghost tours, date nights, bachelor/bachelorette parties, or birthday parties.
  • Website and app were recently revamped.
  • Very active on social media (Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter).
  • Excellent ratings on Google Play and the App Store.

Weaknesses

  • Content of hunts is weak with very basic historical info, and riddles are not very challenging (e.g.: what's the color of the door?)
  • Activating a hunt is convoluted and frustrating.
  • Interactive roles (such as explorer, brainiac, or photographer), don't add anything to the game.
  • No game demo. Users don't know what to expect until they purchase tickets.
  • Let's Roam doesn't address negative reviews.

Strengths

  • Content of quests is excellent: clever riddles, interesting historical facts, and fun photo challenges.
  • Discounts are offered regularly.
  • Game demo and sample riddles allow users to see how it works before purchasing a hunt.
  • UAQ responds to the majority of their reviews, both positive and negative.
  • Customers can get a full refund if not satisfied with the quest, no questions asked.
  • Strong rating on TrustPilot: 4.4 stars out of five.

Weaknesses

  • Quests are only available in the United States (plus 1 city in Canada).
  • Web interface is clunky and looks dated.
  • Price could be a deterrent ($49 per quest for up to 5 people).
  • Playing requires an internet connection.

Opportunities & Business Strategy

As mil­len­ni­als reach the age where they have the means and de­sire to travel, there’s a real op­por­tu­nity to mod­ern­ize the self-guided tour in­dus­try where we can com­bine the con­ve­nience of an app for ex­plor­ing a city, while hav­ing fun with friends and fam­ily.

Here are initial thoughts on StreetCred’s business strategy:

  • Provide users with a memorable sightseeing experience by offering ready-made scavenger hunts with high-quality content (clever riddles, interesting historical info, fun photo challenges) that can be played anytime.
  • Start with cities in the United States to test the concept locally. Then expand to popular travel destinations around the world.
  • Partner with travel agencies and walking tour companies to develop hunts, and encourage contributions from locals in exchange for royalties or a commission.
  • Provide a hunt demo that realistically mimics a hunt so users can get familiar with the game before paying for a hunt.
  • To mitigate frustration when a hunt is not yet available in the desired city, offer free generic hunts (at the zoo or forest preserve, for instance) that are not city-specific. Generic hunts also allow users to test the app before purchasing a city-specific hunt.

USER SURVEYS

Now that we have a good grasp on our competition, we need to understand our users. I decided to start with a survey to gather initial insights from a large sample of users.

The survey consisted of 23 questions (most of them multiple choice), split into 3 manageable sections that aligned with the research goals below.

Travel & Leisure

  • Determine if people want to spend more time outside.
  • Identify what people like to do when they travel.
  • Assess how adventurous people are in terms of trying new things.

Gaming & Social Media

Understand people’s gaming /social media habits and usage:

  • What type of games do people like to play on their phones? Why?
  • How much do people use social media, and for what purpose?

Scavenger Hunts

  • Determine if users would turn to a scavenger hunt game as a way to explore their city, or as a means to discover a new city while traveling.
  • Identify what type of activities or tasks people like to do during a scavenger hunt.
  • Understand to what extent people are interested in historical / cultural information, if at all.

I launched the survey via Google Forms, which I sent to co-workers, friends, friends of friends, fellow students, and a Facebook group of Chicago suburban moms. The survey resulted in a 100% completion rate from 78 respondents.

USER INTERVIEWS

The sur­vey was great for gath­er­ing a large amount of quan­ti­ta­tive in­for­ma­tion about our users. Let’s now turn to in­ter­views to gen­er­ate a dif­fer­ent type of user data: qual­i­ta­tive in­for­ma­tion.

Research Goals

My re­search goals for the in­ter­views are sim­i­lar to the ones I had es­tab­lished for the sur­vey. With in­ter­views how­ever, I have the op­por­tu­nity to dig deeper on each topic. Here are ad­di­tional re­search goals that were not ad­dressed by the sur­vey:

  • Find out about people's experiences when traveling (good and bad).
  • Gather people's thoughts on guided tours and museums.
  • Determine what people know / think about geocaching, Pokémon Go, and escape the room games.
  • Get suggestions for features and activities people would like to have in an urban scavenger hunt game.

Interview Script

Rather than have a rigid script with spe­cific ques­tions, I pre­pared talk­ing points. That ap­proach worked well in keep­ing the in­ter­views con­ver­sa­tional and laid back.

I selected 5 interviewees with the following characteristics:

  • 3 city dwellers + 2 suburbanites.
  • 2 single + 2 married with children + 1 married without children.
  • All enjoy traveling and have traveled more than once in the past year.
  • While not all of them have participated in a scavenger hunt recently, I chose them because they seemed to be the type to enjoy that kind of activity.

KEY INSIGHTS

Yes, the amount of data generated by the survey and interviews can be a little intimidating. But it turned out to be extremely valuable once I organized and categorized it, as it helped me to derive key insights for features to include in the app.

  • Feedback from the survey and interviews strongly supported including riddles, picture challenges and historical information in the hunts.

  • All 5 interviewees mentioned that they would like the hunt to incorporate a mix of famous landmarks , as well as little hidden gems only known by the locals.

  • Most people think a scavenger hunt is a great way to explore a city while traveling. On the other hand, the idea of a generic hunt or playing a hunt in their hometown was not very popular.

  • Very few people want to race during a scavenger hunt; most prefer to take their time and enjoy the sights.

  • Most trav­el­ers want to try lo­cal cui­sine, and all in­ter­vie­wees said they would wel­come restau­rant rec­om­men­da­tions within a scav­enger hunt. But stay away from the big chains!

It’s crucial to understand our users early in the design process. Let’s use the massive amount of info we collected in the research phase and convert it into UX design currency: user personas, mental journeys, and user flows.

WHO ARE WE DESIGNING FOR?

From there, I iden­ti­fied re­cur­ring themes and im­por­tant char­ac­ter­is­tics to cre­ate user per­sonas.

HOW WILL THE APP BE USED?

In or­der to empathize with our users, let’s map out how they will use the app to per­form cer­tain ac­tiv­i­ties. In the fol­low­ing user jour­ney map, we’ll see how Dereck in­ter­acts with the app while in Austin, TX dur­ing spring break. We’ll also ob­serve what he thinks and feels along the way.

This emo­tional roller coaster can help us iden­tify op­por­tuni­ties to re­duce fric­tion points and im­prove the user ex­pe­ri­ence.

Opportunities from Dereck's Journey

  • If location access is allowed, the home screen should automatically display hunts nearby (but still include a search bar) and show the user’s current location on a map relative to nearby hunts.
  • In addition to offering various payment options, there should be a feature to securely save credit card information within the app.
  • Game options should allow users to select which features to include in the hunt (for instance: exclude riddles, but include photo challenges).
  • Given that each hunt is a loop, the starting point of a hunt should be the checkpoint that is closest to the user’s current location, or the user could pick desired starting point from a list.
  • There should be a feature to take pictures from within the app, with a few social media options for the ability to quickly share. All pictures taken within the app should automatically be saved to the photo library (with user’s permission).
  • Users should be able to rate the recommendations so the app can better tailor recommendations over time.

Opportunities from Malika’s and Angie’s Journeys

Ad­di­tional op­por­tu­ni­ties were iden­ti­fied by ob­serv­ing how Ma­lika is us­ing our app as a sub­sti­tute for a guided walk­ing tour in San Fran­cisco, and how Angie re­lies on Street­Cred as a fun ac­tiv­ity to do in her own city with her kids and guests from out town.

  • Businesses we partner with (such as tour companies, restaurants, and museums) should be provided with a QR code so users can easily download the app by scanning the code. The code could also be embedded with a specific hunt the vendor recommends and/or a discount code.
  • Our app should be supported by a website to increase the likelihood it will show up in Google search results. We should also have a Face-book page, and have a strong presence on Instagram and Twitter.
  • Whether from the website or within the app, there should be a feature to save hunts for later, with the ability to set a reminder or add to calendar.
  • Directions to the starting point should be provided in-app, with options to drive, walk, take public transportation, or ride-share.

DOWN TO THE NITTY-GRITTY

The user jour­ney maps were great for iden­ti­fy­ing prob­lems and get­ting us think­ing about po­ten­tial so­lu­tions. Let’s take it one step fur­ther and cre­ate user flows in which we map spe­cific tasks that our users need to ac­com­plish within the app to reach suc­cess­ful com­ple­tion of their goal. Set­ting out that spe­cific path and all de­ci­sions in­volved along the way is use­ful for dis­cov­er­ing what type of screens are needed to ac­com­mo­date the users. That will come in handy when cre­at­ing the sitemap.

DESIGN ITERATIONS

Let’s take a closer look at the core fea­tures of the app to see how the de­sign evolved from pa­per and in­dex cards, to greyscale dig­i­tal wire­frames, to col­or­ful high-fidelity mock­ups that re­sem­ble a real app.

A. Finding a hunt

In early iterations, the hunt cards were on top of the map in a single column format, meaning that when the user scrolls up to view more cards, the map is not visible anymore.

In later screens, the hunt cards and the map don’t overlap anymore. The cards are in a sin­gle row for­mat, al­low­ing the user to swipe left to view the next card while still be­ing able to see the map.

I’m a visual person so I LOVE to be able to see the full-size map at all times. And it’s re­ally cool how the map moves to cen­ter in on the new route when you swipe to see the next hunt at the bottom.

B. Getting info about a hunt

The hunt de­tail started out as a sim­ple screen, with de­tail about the hunt, and a few ac­tions such as buy this hunt, add to cal­en­dar, and start hunt.

I stream­lined the lay­out to establish a clear CTA in the bot­tom nav­i­ga­tion, while pre­sent­ing sec­ondary ac­tions in the form of FABs overlapping the hunt pic­ture.

In the end, the chal­lenge was to pre­sent a large amount of in­for­ma­tion in an or­ga­nized man­ner while mak­ing it easy for the user to iden­tify and find ac­tion­able items.

C. Buying a hunt

The Buy Hunt screen did­n’t change much be­tween low and high fi­delity. How­ever, the pay­ment con­fir­ma­tion screen was ma­jorly im­proved. In the low fi­delity stage, it gave the user too many op­tions af­ter pur­chas­ing a hunt.

Only the most im­por­tant action ‘Start­ scavenger hunt now’ is kept on the con­fir­ma­tion modal, while the rest of the op­tions are avail­able from the Hunt De­tail screen if the user prefers to start the hunt later.

D. Selecting preferences

The idea of us­ing pills for the user to se­lect pref­er­ences stuck till the end (al­though they kind of look like pud­dles in this very low fi­delity version…)

The over­all de­sign got more crisp and re­fined. For instance, I replaced the traditional progress bar with large icons to make the screen look more enticing.

Test­ing with real peo­ple is a cru­cial step in the it­er­a­tive de­sign process, not only to re­fine the de­sign, but also to de­ter­mine how suc­cess­ful we were in solv­ing the ini­tial prob­lem. Are we pro­vid­ing the right fea­tures? Are they us­able? Are they en­joy­able? Let’s find out.

Failing to plan is planning to fail.

USABILITY TEST PLAN

­­I pre­pared a us­abil­ity test plan to out­line the scope of the test­ing and iron out lo­gis­ti­cal de­tails in ad­vance of the ses­sions. Here’s what my plan in­cludes:

IDENTIFYING, PRIORITIZING, AND FIXING ISSUES

I used affin­ity map­ping to or­ga­nize the in­for­ma­tion gath­ered from the test­ing ses­sions into group­ings. I then an­a­lyzed those ob­ser­va­tions, errors, pos­i­tive quotes, and neg­a­tive quotes to gen­er­ate a list of is­sues that I cat­e­go­rized as crit­i­cal, high, medium, or low pri­or­ity.

While only one is­sue was crit­i­cal, I de­cided to also fix the rest of the is­sues with lower pri­or­ity lev­els to elim­i­nate points of fric­tion and pro­vide users with a more pleas­ant ex­pe­ri­ence, in­creas­ing the like­li­hood they will re­turn to Street­Cred to buy and play more hunts when trav­el­ing to U.S. cities.

Below are the top two issues that surfaced from the usability testing, and how I fixed them.

ISSUE #1 | critical

Users don’t want to have to join or log in to buy a hunt

Four of the ­6 testers did­n’t un­der­stand they had to join to pur­chase a hunt and wanted the op­tion to check out as a guest.

Creating an account is a deal-breaker. I don’t want to have to remember another password. Had this been a real app, I wouldn’t have pur­chased the hunt.

—Rich (tester #1)

I don’t mind cre­at­ing a new ac­count, but I’d like to be able to try the app be­fore join­ing. If I can poke around with­out hav­ing to cre­ate an ac­count, it tells me the app has noth­ing to hide.

SOLUTION

Offer the option to checkout as a guest.

Only ask for the min­i­mum info re­quired to check­out and be trans­par­ent about how that info will be used.

En­cour­age users to cre­ate an ac­count AF­TER they have suc­cess­fully paid for a hunt.

ISSUE #2 | medium priority

The Players screen is not very intuitive when it comes to splitting players into teams.

Only 1 of the 6 par­tic­i­pants cre­ated a new team on first click be­fore at­tempt­ing to move play­ers.

Four of the other 5 par­tic­i­pants thought the process was not very in­tu­itive.

One par­tic­i­pant said she would­n't have known play­ers could be split into teams had it not been a task she was asked to per­form.

Users didn’t understand the meaning of the asterisk icon and didn’t notice there were team captains.

SOLUTION

I re-de­signed the screen with clear team con­tain­ers, and added a sec­ond empty team to in­di­cate play­ers can be split into teams.

I changed the asterisk icon to a simple ‘C’ to identify the captains, and added a popup to point out what the icon means and how captains can be changed.

I’m not the type to read in­struc­tions; I just jump in and fig­ure things out on my own. It’s nice that the app is point­ing out the cap­tains, but I’m not in­ter­ested in the FAQ right now. I’m sure the cap­tains will make sense when I start the hunt. If it’s still un­clear, then I know to go to the FAQ for an­swers.

Accessibility

  • contrast & readability
  • beyond contrast

I aim for my designs to be accessible, inclusive, and non-discriminatory, and StreetCred is no exception. As such, I went over every single screen with a fine-tooth comb to check for readability.

CONTRAST & READABILITY

I targeted a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 to meet WCAG’s AA conformance level. In some cases, I increased the contrast ratio even if it already met the AA standard because the user will have to read a sig­nif­i­cant amount of text when play­ing a hunt.

ACCESSIBILITY BEYOND CONTRAST

Accessibility is so much more than contrast and readability. For instance, rather than using color to convey information, it should be used to complement information.

A color-blind person can tell there’s an error here because they can see the in-field icon and they can read the error message.

Users can see the difference between the two buttons regardless of color because the primary button is filled, while the other is a ghost button.

Links are easy to differentiate from regular text based on more than color because they are underlined.

An­other way to make de­sign ac­ces­si­ble is to have bor­ders around in­put fields so the user can tell where they’re ex­pected to en­ter text. Ad­di­tion­ally place­holder text is used to sup­ple­ment the la­bel, not to re­place it.

The user is able to tell what the field is even when the placeholder text is not available anymore because the label is outside of the field and therefore always visible.

Final Thoughts

It was quite a journey to start with a problem to solve and end up with a scavenger hunt app that allows users to find hunts, read details about a hunt, bookmark a hunt, buy a hunt, invite other players to join in on the fun, split players into teams, and set preferences to obtain tailored recommendations.

FUTURE IMPROVEMENTS

After developing 5 core features and designing 30 different screens, I feel like I’ve only scratched the surface. There’s so much more I’m planning on doing with StreetCred!

I’d like to give the users the abil­ity to add a hunt to their cal­en­dar, whether the hunt is pur­chased or not. I be­lieve this would be a use­ful fea­ture for peo­ple who like to plan their trip ac­tiv­i­ties in ad­vance.

Every­thing I’ve de­vel­oped so far hap­pens be­fore play­ing a hunt. Now I want to al­low users to ac­tu­ally play a hunt. That’s a huge un­der­tak­ing as it in­volves de­vel­op­ing a sub-app within the app. I’m up for the chal­lenge!

I want to fine-tune the flow for joining or logging in, and explore an option where the user could join or log in without having to create a password.

Once the func­tion­al­ity to play a hunt is developed, users need a way to ac­cess their pre­vi­ously played hunts so they can see pic­tures taken dur­ing the hunt, re­fer to the his­tor­i­cal in­for­ma­tion pro­vided, and re­trieve rec­om­men­da­tions and dis­counts of­fered.

I’d love to de­velop the scav­enger hunt demo. It’s an im­por­tant fea­ture for first-time users so they know what to ex­pect be­fore they in­vest their money and pre­cious va­ca­tion time into a hunt.

LEARNINGS

I thor­oughly en­joyed learn­ing about the many facets of the UX de­sign process while work­ing on this pro­ject. Now armed with hind­sight, there are a few things I will do dif­fer­ently in fu­ture pro­jects:

  • Use index cards to sketch low-fidelity wireframes.

  • Be mindful of accessibility early in the process.

  • Develop a design system as soon as I start designing high-fidelity mockups.

  • Despite my best efforts to put myself in the user's shoes,
    I am not the user.
    That’s why it’s so important to get users involved as much as possible throughout the design process with the many tools and techniques at our disposal, such as surveys, interviews, card sorting, usability testing, preference tests, first-click test, etc.

  • In future projects, I'd like to do more rounds of usability testing, and leverage tree-testing, preference tests and first-click tests.
